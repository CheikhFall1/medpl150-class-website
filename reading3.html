“Graphic Novel: Motherhood in crisis”

By Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager and Saidu Bah

Reading “Graphic Novel: Motherhood in Crisis” honestly made me think about how pregnancy is treated so differently depending on where someone lives. In some places, pregnancy is already stressful, but there is still the expectation that hospitals, doctors, transportation, and emergency care will be available. In the stories from Sierra Leone, pregnancy feels like something that can become life-threatening very quickly. What stood out to me is that the women were not only dealing with pregnancy itself. They were also dealing with distance, poverty, weak healthcare systems, and the fear that help might not come in time.

The graphic novel format made the topic feel more personal. If this was written as a normal article with only statistics, I probably would have understood the issue, but I might not have felt it the same way. The illustrations slow the reader down and make each woman’s story feel more human. You can actually picture the fear, waiting, pain, and uncertainty. It also makes the issue easier to follow because maternal health can sometimes feel like a huge global problem that is hard to fully understand. By focusing on individual women, the reading shows that this is not just about numbers. It is about real people trying to survive something that should not have to be so dangerous.

This connects directly to what we talk about in class about media shaping perception. Images and media have always influenced how we see the world, but now the scale is completely different. Before, editing a photo or creating fake media required skill and effort. Now it can be done in seconds. That changes how much power individuals have to manipulate information. If anyone can generate a realistic fake event, then proof does not mean the same thing anymore. That is honestly kind of unsettling.

What bothered me the most was how much of the crisis seems preventable. The problem is not that pregnancy is automatically supposed to be deadly. The problem is that many women do not have proper access to care when they need it. That connects to human rights because healthcare should not depend so much on where someone is born or how much money they have. A woman should not have to risk her life just because the nearest clinic is too far, too crowded, or not equipped enough. Reading this made me think about how basic healthcare is tied to dignity. If society values women’s lives, then maternal healthcare has to be treated as a serious priority.

I also thought about motherhood differently after reading this. A lot of the time, people talk about motherhood like it is only beautiful or natural, but this reading shows the danger and pressure that can come with it. These women are strong, but I do not think the point is just to praise their strength. Sometimes people call women strong and then ignore why they had to be that strong in the first place. The real issue is that no woman should have to face childbirth with that much fear and that little support.

Overall, “Motherhood in Crisis” was powerful because it made a global health issue feel close and human. It showed me that maternal health is not just a medical issue. It is also a human rights issue, a gender issue, and a justice issue. The reading made it clear that survival should not come down to luck, distance, or whether a healthcare system is strong enough on that specific day.
